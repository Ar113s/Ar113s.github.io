Let’s be honest—books aren’t everyone’s first choice. They can feel slow, boring, even outdated compared to fast-paced YouTube tutorials and flashy courses.

But that’s exactly why they matter.

Videos make things look easy. You follow along, copy commands, and get results—but most of the time, you don’t really understand what’s happening behind the scenes.

Books force you to slow down and think. They break things apart, explain the logic, and help you understand systems at a deeper level. Instead of just running tools, you start understanding how they actually work.

Before you get into hacking and breaking things, you need to understand what a system is made of and how it functions. So, I recommend starting with a basic computer crash course. This will help you understand how computers work and how they evolved into what we use today. A good starting point is this:

[Crash Course](https://youtu.be/tpIctyqH29Q), This course helped me a lot and if you want you can do professor messer [comptia+](https://youtu.be/87t6P5ZHTP0) too.

After that, move on to Networking—or learn it in parallel with above courses. You need to understand how data travels between systems and how those signals can be used or manipulated. Networking is the backbone of modern technology. No matter how powerful something like AI becomes, without networking, it’s useless—like a locked system with no way in or out.

- [Network basics for hackers](/assets/files/Network_basics_for_hackers.pdf)-- A beginner-friendly introduction to networking concepts such as IP addresses, ports, protocols, and how data moves between systems, forming the foundation for cybersecurity.

Next, learn Linux. It’s one of the most important operating systems in cybersecurity. Many security tools are built for it, and it gives you far more control compared to Windows or macOS. Distributions like Fedora, Kali, and Parrot are all built around it, and it’s widely used because of its flexibility and open-source nature.

- [Linux basics for hackers](/assets/files/Linux%20Basics%20for%20Hackers_%20Getting%20Started%20with%20Networking,%20Scripting,%20and%20Security%20in%20Kali%20(%20PDFDrive%20).pdf)-- A beginner-friendly guide to learning Linux fundamentals, including commands, file systems, and basic scripting, with a focus on cybersecurity use cases.

Once you’ve covered these basics, you can start getting into cybersecurity. This is where you begin learning how systems are tested, how vulnerabilities are found, and how access is gained.

- [Becoming master hacker](/assets/files/becoming-master-hacker.pdf)-- A general overview of hacking concepts and techniques, but not very structured. Best used for casual reading, not as a primary learning source.
- [Penetration testing](/assets/files/Penetration%20Testing%20-%20A%20hands-on%20introduction%20to%20Hacking.pdf)-- A hands-on guide that introduces real-world penetration testing concepts, tools, and workflows from reconnaissance to exploitation.
- [Hacking the art of exploitation](/assets/files/Hacking%20the%20art%20of%20exploitation.pdf)-- Focuses on low-level concepts like memory, C programming, and exploit development. More advanced and best read after building strong basics.
- [Hacker playbook 1](/assets/files/The%20Hacker%20Playbook_%20Practical%20Guide%20To%20Penetration%20Testing%20(%20PDFDrive%20).pdf)-- A practical guide with real-world attack strategies, tools, and techniques used in penetration testing and red teaming.

Also, don’t ignore Python. With all the AI hype, a lot of people skip learning programming—but that’s a mistake. You don’t need to master it immediately, but you should at least understand the basics. Many tools and scripts you’ll find online aren’t plug-and-play. You’ll need to tweak them, fix errors, or modify them to fit your needs.

- [Python crash course](/assets/files/Python%20crash%20course.pdf)-- A beginner-friendly book that teaches the fundamentals of Python programming.
- [Black hat python](/assets/files/Black%20Hat%20Python.pdf)-- Covers Python concepts specifically used in cybersecurity, including building tools and scripts.
- [Automating the boaring stuff with python](/assets/files/Automate%20The%20Boring%20Stuff%20With%20Python.pdf)-- Shows how to use Python to automate everyday tasks and explains practical functions used in real-world scenarios.

And that’s it for now. I’ve got plenty more books to recommend, but this is more than enough to get you started.

> Try to read the books in the same order as i posted within the pentesting and python category
